What It Is

CHANGE EVERYTHING.
DAMAGE NOTHING.

A color change wrap is a full-vehicle vinyl film installation that replaces the visual appearance of your factory paint — without touching a single panel. We cover every inch: roof, hood, doors, bumpers, mirrors, pillars. The result looks better than most paint jobs and costs 60% less.

Unlike paint, it's reversible. Peel it off in 5 years, and your factory paint is in the same condition it was on the day we installed. That preserves resale value. No paint booth. No cure time. No permanent commitment.

Chicago Fleet Wraps uses only premium cast vinyl — Avery Dennison MPI 1105 and 3M IJ180-CV3. These films are rated 5–7 years outdoor. They conform to curves, door handles, and recessed channels without lifting or bubbling. We don't touch calendered vinyl — it shrinks, lifts, and fails within 18 months.

After the Install

HOW TO MAKE IT LAST 7 YEARS

A color change wrap on cast vinyl is durable — but it's not maintenance-free. The difference between a wrap that looks sharp at year 6 and one that starts failing at year 3 comes down to how it's washed and stored.

✓
HAND WASH ONLY

Touchless or hand wash only. Brush car washes abrade the surface and lift edges over time.

✓
KEEP PRESSURE BELOW 1,200 PSI

High-pressure washers at close range lift edges at seams. Stay at least 18 inches from the surface.

✓
VINYL-SAFE PRODUCTS ONLY

No wax on matte or satin finishes — it creates shiny spots. Use 3M Car Wash Soap or a vinyl-specific product.

✓
INDOOR STORAGE EXTENDS LIFE

UV exposure and freeze-thaw cycles are the primary lifespan reducers. Garage storage can add 1–2 years to a wrap's life in Chicago's climate.

✓
CATCH LIFT EDGES EARLY

If an edge starts lifting, address it immediately. A minor re-tuck is a 5-minute fix. Ignored, it becomes a peeled panel in two weeks.

CHICAGO-SPECIFIC CONSIDERATIONS

Chicago's climate is one of the harshest in the country for vehicle wraps. Here's what that means for your install:

  • Road salt: Rinse the vehicle weekly in winter. Salt accelerates edge lift.
  • Temperature cycling: Cast vinyl handles Chicago winters — calendered does not.
  • UV in summer: Clear coat or sacrificial film over high-sun panels extends color life.
  • Parking: Avoid parking on pavement that reflects heat onto lower body panels.

Will the wrap damage my paint? +
No — and with cast vinyl it actually protects your paint. Cast vinyl film acts as a sacrificial barrier against UV degradation, rock chips, minor abrasion, and environmental contaminants. When removed by a professional (which we do for free during reinstalls), the factory paint underneath is in better condition than if it had been left exposed. The only risk of paint damage occurs when cheap calendered vinyl or improper removal techniques are used. We use neither.
How long does a color change wrap last? +
With cast vinyl — Avery Dennison MPI 1105 or 3M IJ180-CV3 — you can expect 5–7 years of outdoor performance. In Chicago's climate with proper care (hand wash, indoor storage where possible, salt rinses in winter), many of our installs reach 6–7 years looking sharp. Calendered vinyl degrades in 18–24 months. We don't use it.
Is matte black or satin black better? +
Both are excellent. Matte black is flat with zero reflectivity — it reads as raw and aggressive. Satin black has a soft sheen that shifts slightly in different light — it reads as refined and premium. Satin is easier to maintain because fingerprints and water spots are less visible. Matte shows imperfections and contact marks more clearly. For daily drivers, satin black is usually the better choice. For show builds and statement vehicles, matte tends to photograph better.
Can I wrap a leased vehicle? +
Yes. A professionally installed and removed color change wrap leaves the factory paint in the same condition it was before the wrap. This is one of the primary reasons color change wraps are popular with leased vehicles — you can transform the car for the duration of the lease and have it removed before turn-in. Check your lease agreement for any restrictions, but the wrap itself creates no paint damage when removed correctly.

A full color change wrap covers all exterior painted surfaces: hood, roof, doors, fenders, trunk/tailgate, bumpers, mirrors, pillars, and roof rails. We also wrap into door jambs for a clean finish. HOW TO CHOOSE YOUR COLOR & FINISH

Most people walk in knowing their vehicle and walk out knowing their color — but the finish type is where the real decision happens. Here's what years of installs have taught us about what works, what doesn't, and what makes people stop in a parking lot.

BY VEHICLE TYPE

SPORTS CARS & MUSCLE

Gloss or color-shift. Sports cars are designed to be seen, and gloss finishes amplify every bodyline. Color-shift adds drama on curves. Avoid matte on vehicles with complex body sculpting — it softens the visual impact of lines that were designed to reflect light.

TRUCKS & PICKUPS

Matte black or matte military finishes dominate truck wraps in Chicago. The flat surface texture matches the utilitarian attitude of the vehicle. Satin black is the alternative when the owner wants the truck to look premium rather than tactical. Avoid color-shift on trucks — it reads as out of context.

SUVS & CROSSOVERS

Satin black or satin gray are the most versatile SUV choices — they look at home in a suburban driveway and in downtown Chicago. Gloss white or gloss pearl give an upscale look on luxury SUVs. Metallics work well on larger SUV bodies where the flake has room to catch light.

LUXURY & EXECUTIVE VEHICLES

Satin finishes pair best with luxury vehicles — they read as intentional and premium without the showmanship of gloss. Satin black on a BMW, Mercedes, or Cadillac looks like a factory option that wasn't available. Always add chrome delete on luxury vehicles — it completes the transformation.

ELECTRIC VEHICLES

Matte finishes are the dominant choice for EVs in Chicago — Tesla, Rivian, Polestar. The understated, non-reflective surface matches the quiet performance character of electric vehicles. We've done 600+ Rivian installations alone. Satin white on white-bodied EVs is also extremely popular for a clean, factory-upgrade appearance.

BY LIFESTYLE & USE

DAILY DRIVER

Satin or gloss — both hold up better to daily contact than matte. Matte shows fingerprints, minor surface marks, and dust more visibly. For a vehicle that gets used hard every day, satin is the practical choice: it still looks sharp but hides daily wear much better.

WEEKEND / SHOW USE

Matte or color-shift. Show vehicles are detailed before each appearance — so matte's higher maintenance isn't a factor. These finishes photograph best and generate the most reaction in person. Color-shift wraps are consistently the most photographed and shared on social media.

BUSINESS / FLEET BRANDING

Solid gloss colors — especially white, black, red, or brand-matched colors. Fleet vehicles need to look consistent across a 5–10 year lifespan and across multiple units. Solid gloss is the most durable, most reproducible, and most professional-looking option for business fleets.

LEASED VEHICLE

Any finish works on a leased vehicle — the wrap protects the factory paint during the lease term and is removed at turn-in. Many lease customers choose bolder finishes they wouldn't commit to in paint, knowing the transformation is temporary. The factory finish underneath is often in better condition after the wrap is removed.

RIDESHARE / GIGS

Gloss black or deep gloss colors make rideshare vehicles look executive-class at a fraction of the cost. Multiple drivers and constant use means you want a finish that wipes clean easily — gloss is the right choice. Some rideshare drivers also add subtle commercial branding alongside their color change.

The Technical Side

CAST VINYL VS. CALENDERED: WHY IT MATTERS FOR COLOR CHANGE WRAPS

The single most important material decision in a color change wrap is the type of vinyl film. There are two: cast vinyl and calendered vinyl. The difference between them determines whether your wrap looks sharp at year 5 or starts failing at year 18 months.

Cast vinyl is manufactured by casting liquid PVC onto a release liner and curing it in a controlled environment. The result is a dimensionally stable film with internal memory that allows it to conform to complex curves, recesses, and riveted surfaces without lifting or shrinking. Cast vinyl is rated 5–7 years outdoor. It's what Avery Dennison MPI 1105 and 3M IJ180-CV3 are.

Calendered vinyl is manufactured by rolling PVC through a series of heated rollers under high pressure — a process that introduces internal stress into the film. When calendered vinyl is installed on a complex curved surface and exposed to Chicago's freeze-thaw temperature cycles, it attempts to return to its original flat shape. The result is shrinkage, lifting, bubbling, and seam separation within 18–24 months.

Calendered vinyl costs 30–40% less than cast. Shops that use it either don't know the difference or are cutting margins. We use only cast vinyl — Avery Dennison and 3M, both manufacturer-certified. There's no version of our work that involves calendered material.
