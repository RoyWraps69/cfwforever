Cast Vinyl vs Calendered Vinyl — The Material Difference That Determines Wrap Life

The difference between cast and calendered vinyl is not a marketing distinction. It is a physical manufacturing difference with real-world consequences for your fleet. Cast vinyl is manufactured by casting liquid PVC onto a flat surface and curing it — this process relieves internal stresses and creates a dimensionally stable film that conforms to curves and survives 5–7 years. Calendered vinyl is extruded under pressure — it retains internal stresses that cause shrinkage, lifting, and premature failure, especially on curved panels and in temperature extremes.

What Calendered Vinyl Failure Looks Like

Calendered vinyl wraps fail in predictable ways: edges start lifting within 1–2 years, especially at bumpers, door handles, and curved surfaces. The material shrinks in cold Chicago winters, pulling away from panel edges. Color fades significantly faster than cast vinyl under UV exposure. For a commercial fleet vehicle that needs to look professional for 5+ years, calendered vinyl is a false economy — you pay less per sqft but you replace the wrap 2–3 times in the same period, spending more overall while looking unprofessional in the interim.
